INTRO

Biomimicry has increasingly influenced the field of robotics, particularly in the development of agile, efficient, and adaptive underwater vehicles. Among the many organisms studied for inspiration, manta rays (genus Mobula) stand out due to their graceful locomotion and unique hydrodynamic body structure. Their broad, wing-like pectoral fins enable efficient propulsion and high maneuverability, making them ideal templates for aquatic robot design.

The objective of this project is to research, design, and develop a bio-inspired robotic manta ray that accurately simulates the motion of a real manta ray using modern prototyping tools. The robotic system not only mimics the anatomical structure of the manta ray but also seeks to capture its fluid swimming behaviour through articulated fin movement driven by servo motors. This portfolio outlines the systematic development process, from concept to physical prototype, highlighting the design decisions, technologies used, and challenges encountered along the way.

Engineering Translation

The first step was to translate the biological principles into a mechanical solution. A flexible yet controllable structure was needed to replicate the wave-like motion. This was approached using segmented wing structures tethered with flexible joints and controlled via servo motors and fishing thread to simulate body contractions.

The mechanical goals included:

  • Reproducing smooth wave-like fin motion.
  • Maintaining hydrodynamic form.
  • Ensuring modularity for testing and assembly & designing a lightweight, watertight shell.

A comprehensive research on manta ray anatomy was done to understand the biomechanics of their movement. Manta rays use dorsoventral undulations of their large, wing-like fins to propel themselves, creating lift-based propulsion similar to bird flight. Their skeleton is composed largely of cartilage, providing flexibility and reduced weight. The design tried it's best to tranlate into a working prototype.

Evaluation & Testing

The prototype was tested and Evaluation criteria included:

  • Motion realism

  • Buoyancy and balance

  • Structural integrity

  • Responsiveness to servo commands

  • The articulated wings produced a convincing wave motion

  • Servo response was consistent, but still required improvement

  • Body was rigid than expected


Limitations

  • Motion was limited to horizontal undulations; vertical pitch control was not implemented

  • Weight distribution affected stability future iterations could benefit from foam buoyancy

  • Fins were unstable in the middle and was not able to hold up the resting position properly

  • Resin was not implemented onto the body, and it looked plasticky


Future Improvements

  • Fully waterproofed servos or magnetic coupling for underwater operation

  • Integration of IMU sensors for motion feedback

  • Adding a dorsal fin for yaw control

  • Wireless control via Bluetooth or RF modules

  • Soft robotics materials (e.g., silicone) for seamless motion

  • Full Resin realistic finishing can be implemented

CONCLUSION

This project effectively demonstrates the feasibility and creative potential of developing a bio-inspired robotic manta ray through a multidisciplinary approach that blends biology, mechanical engineering, electronics, and digital fabrication. By integrating advanced tools such as 3D scanning, CAD modeling (Onshape), motion capture, haptic sculpting, and resin finishing, a realistic and functionally articulated prototype was developed to simulate the graceful motion of a manta ray. Despite limitations—such as incomplete waterproofing and restricted underwater operation—the prototype achieved its primary goal of replicating the fluid wing motion typical of a real manta ray using servo-driven mechanical actuation. The project also served as a valuable proof of concept, showcasing how biological inspiration and iterative engineering can converge to create robotic systems that mimic natural locomotion. Furthermore, the development process underscored the importance of hands-on prototyping, material experimentation, and the synergy between software and physical tools. This foundation opens up avenues for future refinement, including enhanced motion dynamics, full waterproofing, and autonomous control—potentially contributing to advancements in biomimetic underwater robotics, educational tools, or even environmental monitoring technologies.
